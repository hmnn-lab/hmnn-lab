Clinical Phenomenology of Movement Disorders

Movement disorders are clinical syndromes that are characterized by either an excess or a paucity of voluntary or involuntary movements that are unrelated to spasticity or weakness. Some of the most common movement disorders include Parkinson’s disease, essential tremor and dystonia. Clinical phenomenology is the careful study and analysis of normal and abnormal movements, and motor control. It is the first and the most crucial step in the diagnosis of movement disorders. Unravelling the physiological mechanisms that underlie clinical phenomenology is key to elucidating the pathophysiology of these disorders and further development of novel treatment strategies. Movement disorders are best studied using neurophysiological methods complemented by neuroimaging and noninvasive brain stimulation. Understanding the neural basis of the clinical phenomenology of movement disorders is the primary research interest of the lab. We specifically focus on identifying the distinct functional brain networks associated with different motor and cognitive deficits that occur in patients with movement disorders, and further modulating them differentially for development of personalized treatment strategies. For this, we use a multimodal approach integrating functional neuroimaging (M/EEG and MRI) and noninvasive brain stimulation methods (transcranial magnetic/electrical stimulation – TMS/tES) with clinical and behavioral outcomes.

Non-invasive Neuromodulation

Although the mechanisms of action of noninvasive neuromodulatory techniques such as transcranial brain stimulation and neurofeedback training remain unclear, their potential for research and clinical applications cannot be underestimated. In our lab, we systematically investigate the impact of noninvasive neuromodulatory techniques such as transcranial magnetic/electrical stimulation and neurofeedback training on behavior in both healthy individuals and patients. We employ these neuromodulatory techniques as tools for understanding the physiological basis of behavior and for modulating the same. Eventually, our aim is to develop neuromodulatory strategies to alleviate cognitive deficits that are left untreated or arise as a side effect of pharmacological therapy in patients with neuropsychiatric disorders.
